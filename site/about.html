About the Project

Author & Project Story

Stele is an independent research software project by Jaehwan Kim, built around proof checking, semantic diagnostics, and the trust boundary of mathematical reasoning.

This page describes what was built, why, and what the evidence is. The artifact, tests, documentation, and whitepaper are the primary record; this page provides context.

Project Story

My interest in mathematical reasoning starts with a question that sounds philosophical but has a precise technical answer: when we say a proof is valid, valid under what rules? Classical logic and intuitionistic logic agree on most inference steps but diverge on a few — double-negation elimination, the law of excluded middle, proof by contradiction. The same formula can be provable in one system and unprovable in another, and that isn't ambiguity: it's a fact about rule sets.

I wanted to explore this concretely, with running code rather than abstract descriptions. The question became: can I build a small, auditable checker where switching the logic is a runtime parameter, not a reimplementation? Where the checker is simple enough to read and trust, but expressive enough to show the classical/intuitionistic difference working?

That question led to Stele's core design: a trusted kernel (stele/kernel.py) that performs only syntactic rule-schema matching, with rule sets as pure data objects (stele/logic.py). Adding a logic means adding data, not modifying the verifier. The kernel stays small and auditable; the rule sets are independently inspectable.

From there the project grew in layers: structural diagnostics that locate proof errors without being trusted with correctness; a Kripke countermodel search that demonstrates why intuitionistic logic rejects certain formulas; a proof-term core connecting the proof language to the Curry–Howard correspondence; matrix semantics (K3, LP, Boolean) for comparing rules across many-valued interpretations; and finally a browser-local Studio that runs the entire checker in Pyodide/WebAssembly with no backend.

The deeper theoretical motivation — a personal research program called Yurihak (유리학) — investigates logical rule-relativity as a foundational question. That program isn't implemented as a Stele logic yet; it's the research horizon that shaped the multi-logic architecture. See the Foundations page for the full picture of what is implemented, what is motivated, and what is planned.

Proof dependency graph Stable

Kripke countermodel search Experimental
Searches Kripke frames (up to a configurable bound) for countermodels to intuitionistic propositional logic formulas. Shows why LEM and double-negation elimination are not IPL-provable. Bounded search — no countermodel found does not imply the formula is valid.
stele/kripke.py

K3 / LP / Boolean matrix semantics Demo
Many-valued matrix evaluators (strong Kleene, Logic of Paradox, classical Boolean) with a rule-soundness checker. Demonstrates the separation between syntactic derivability (⊢) and semantic validity (⊨). LEM is classically provable but not a K3 tautology.

Proof-term core / Curry–Howard Experimental
An experimental proof-term calculus for intuitionistic propositional logic: bidirectional type checking, β-reduction, de Bruijn indices, and script-to-term elaboration. Connects verified proof scripts to typed proof witnesses.

Proof certificates & minicheck Experimental

My Role

I designed the project direction, architecture, and claim boundaries. The core decisions — what the trusted kernel may and may not do, how rule sets are separated from the verifier, what status labels to attach to each feature, what counts as evidence — are mine.

I used AI coding tools as implementation assistants throughout development. The scope of each feature, the testing requirements, the honesty constraints (no invented metrics, no overclaimed metatheory), and the public-release review workflow were my responsibility. Every accepted output was checked through the test suite and manually reviewed against the stated design invariants.

Concretely: I wrote the formal specification in docs/semantics.md, the metatheory claims and their status in docs/metatheory.md, the design decisions in DECISIONS.md, the claim-evidence matrix in docs/research-notes/claim-evidence-matrix.md, and the provenance map in docs/provenance-map.md. I audited every public claim on the site and in the whitepaper for accuracy and appropriate hedging.

The project is independent — no institutional affiliation, no team, no funding. It grew from a personal interest in the formal structure of logical reasoning and a desire to build something precise and auditable rather than large and impressive-looking.

Research Motivation

Stele's multi-logic architecture is motivated by a personal foundational research program — Yurihak (유리학) — which investigates the conditions under which logical rule systems, semantic frameworks, and proof obligations are themselves relative to a choice of foundations. The question isn't "which logic is correct" but "what is it to choose a logic, and what follows from that choice."

Stele demonstrates one concrete aspect of this: that logical validity is relative to the chosen rule set. The same formula is provable under classical rules and not provable under intuitionistic rules. That's implemented, testable, and runs in a browser. The deeper formalization of Yurihak as a Stele logic is future work.

Scope note

Yurihak is research motivation, not a feature of the current software. Stele does not implement Yurihak as a formal logic and does not claim to prove philosophical theses about logical relativism. See the Foundations page for the full implemented / motivation / future separation.

Limitations

What Stele is not
  • Not a theorem prover. Stele checks proofs you write; it does not search for proofs.
  • Not a Lean, Rocq/Coq, or Isabelle replacement. Those systems have dependent types, tactic languages, large theorem libraries, and machine-checked metatheory. Stele is propositional.
  • Not a full first-order logic system. The proof-script surface is propositional. FOL is available at the proof-term level only (experimental).
  • Metatheory is not machine-checked. Claims about subject reduction, normalization, and consistency are supported by regression tests and proof sketches — not by Lean/Coq/Agda verification.
  • Classical bridge is formula-level only. λμ-calculus / continuation typing for classical proof terms is not implemented.
  • Kripke search is bounded. Finding no countermodel does not prove the formula is IPL-valid.
  • ML baseline is optional and untrusted. A small synthetic corpus, Naive Bayes, not representative of real proof corpora.
  • Browser deployment needs Pyodide CDN (~8 MB). Not fully offline.

For a full limitations list with future-work mapping, see docs/research-notes/11-limitations-and-future-work.md ↗ .
